 We love the World Clock app and have it set to show the times for Caitlyn's friends around the world that she Skypes with. We've talked about time differences in minutes and hours and how when she's on at midnight. With her friends in Australia it's morning for them,

It's just a part of everyday use, not some abstract calculation that someone else thinks is important to figure out :)

lucy.web at 2013-10-23 00:18:25 +0300 said:

My 10 year old daughter and I were watching Brat Camp UK on Youtube. She is fascinated by these sorts of 'kids behaving badly' programmes. I mentioned that soon I would have to go out and shut the chickens away. She looked at the progress bar, which showed that we were something like 26.45 minutes into a 47.24 programme. "We're over half way," she said. "There's only just over 20 minutes to go."

Mental subtraction of decimals, conversion to fractions AND estimating all in one go. I remember estimation tripped me up for *ages* in school ... couldn't understand why we were being asked to 'guess' something when they'd already shown us how to work it out exactly. It never made sense for me until I actually had to estimate something out in the real world. By starting off with the real world applications, hopefully my daughters will never have to suffer the general foggy confusion over maths that I did.

Lucy

Here are two examples from my sons, ages 7 and 5:

 

A few months ago, my sons and I were hanging out in our front room, the 5 year-old on the computer, the 7 year-old watching TV from the comfy couch, and I reading on my Kindle.  My 7 year-old asks, "Mommy, what's 2 times 19?"  I answer, "38."  My son then says, "So, if that guy pays 38 dollars, he can keep it for 2 days."  I ask, "What guy?" thinking I missed something that just happened on the show my son was watching.  My son then pulls aside the curtain of our big front window, and says, "That guy -- with the pickup truck."  Turns out there was a U-Haul rental pickup parked in front of our house with a big Rent Me for $19 sign on the side (smaller print, of course, showed an additional 95 cents + tax, but I still thought that was pretty cool).

chris ester at 2013-11-09 18:13:41 +0200 said:

My son (17) learned to calculate thrust ratios for a rocketry simulation game.  Gerber Space something or other... 

When he was working on it, I asked if he wanted any help (his father was a math minor and I took lots of math in school/college) and he replied that he felt it was easier to find resources on the net because even if he couldn't articulate the exact question, he would know which information was useful when he saw it.   

I have to admit that a small part of me felt disappointed (I enjoyed when we would team up to find answers or I could explain something or other to him), but a larger part was immensely happy that my son is such a confident living-learning-living guy.  

I have adult friends who are very intelligent and can pinpoint the exact moment when they became fearful of math in school because of 'failure' in a math class.  An unschooled person learns what they want when they want to, and not a minute sooner; and this usually follows some perceived need or desire to get the answer to a particular question... 

My daughter (15) has recently decided that she will get a GED in the future (she doesn't have to, but feels it will be the easiest path to her goals).  She did some research and found some prep tests and these indicated that her math skills needed some improvement.  She did some more research and found Kahn academy and has been working through that to get to whatever level will enable her to pass the math portion of the GED.  

I found out all of this when I saw her writing out long division problems and asked what she was working on.  Her plan, her research, her success.  I love it when a plan comes together.... :)
Chris
