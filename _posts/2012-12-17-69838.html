I saw Shaun the Sheep mentioned and thought I'd say something about that
one. Kai fell in love with it several months ago. At first, as I watched
it, I thought it was really simple. But I've discovered that it's really
useful for dealing with social situations and observing body language. None
of the animals speak a common language, really, at least not one that we
could understand. So it is based on what you see, rather than what you hear
(except for tone). We've gotten a lot out of that, discussing, "Oh, did he
look happy? Or did he look sad?" Anyway, I just thought I'd give a plug for
that one - it's deeper than it looks.

- Misa

My kids are 4.5 and 7.5.

As mentioned by someone else, they also LOVE the Backyardigans (incredibly clever music and dance work if you watch it) and Curious George.

We just finished Avatar: The Last Airbender, which both of them loved. They want to watch the whole series again from the start.

Both of them also like Franklin (although it isn't on Netflix in Canada anymore, sadly, nor is Curious George, although you can get it on iTunes).

My daughter likes Wallace and Grommit, although my son doesn't.

They both like The Wind in the Willows...you can order the first two seasons and the two feature length films made by the BBC through Amazon. For some reason, the remaining seasons were never issued on DVD.

Both kids also like Babar, although probably not as much as the others I have mentioned.

Also popular in our house are classic Looney Tunes - we have a collection on DVD which both find fun to watch. There is so much historical context in those older cartoons it is funny the discussions we have, like, what are those little things in the corners of the drawers that look like Hershey Kisses? (Shelf paper weights, if you are wondering!) There are lots of neat little references that need explaining too, like why would the grandmother be working swing at the factory? Things like that.
