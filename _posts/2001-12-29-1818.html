Let me see, if I remember my theatre history... Yep. Inigo was quite the
theatrical artist. Since I studied design for theatre (not history where I
got some of my lowest grades), I know of him for his design work, costumes,
sets, and most of all effects. He just loved those effects. Exploding staffs
of silk anything, pyrotechnics, reversing costumes, concealed doors and
traps. I lot of what we see today in magicians acts were originally stylized
by Inigo way back in the early 1600s. If you look him up in the history
books I'm sure they would talk about his architecture. I think he was
architect to the king or someone really important.

These masques started off as a kind of costume ball of ceremonial dances
that went crazy and eventually was presented at court and in public
theatres. The more attention one could gather at a social event the better
chance at whatever you would have with the court. So Inigo perfected how to
get attention. I guess it paid off in his architecture career. He could
design sets, costumes, and to a small degree-lighting, he wrote many of the
elaborate masques, directed and even performed in them . You might even call
him a ......Renaissance Man... <G>

-Dan Vilter
